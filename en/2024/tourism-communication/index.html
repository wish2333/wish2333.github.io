Exploring Practice Paths for Cultural Square Empowerment in Rural Revitalization Under the Context of Cultural and Tourism Communication--An Investigation Based on Feng Village, a National Key Rural Tourism Site in Guangxi, China

Abstract

With the proposition of rural revitalization and the rise of cultural and tourism industries, the demand for rural image construction and dissemination has increasingly become prominent. Rural cultural squares, as essential components of novel public spaces, have yet to receive in-depth scrutiny regarding their close relationship with rural revitalization strategies and their roles in cultural and tourism communication. This paper takes rural cultural squares as its research subject and, through an investigation of Feng Village in Guangxi Province, reveals that rural cultural squares have gradually evolved into pivotal public gathering places and objects of cultural and tourism communication in Feng Village. They enrich the villagers’ cultural lives and serve as a window for tourists to understand the culture of Feng Village. However, issues concerning power representation inherent in the development of cultural squares and their inadequacies in meeting the demands of cultural and tourism communication are also noteworthy.

This paper emphasizes the importance of employing strategies from three dimensions – memory empowerment, activity empowerment, and digital empowerment – to enhance the empowering function of cultural squares. By integrating these strategies, rural cultural squares can better contribute to rural revitalization efforts and optimize their role in the broader context of cultural and tourism dissemination.

Research Origins

Rural public cultural spaces serve as the carrier domains where villagers engage in public life and constitute a crucial aspect of rural image construction. With the growing significance of rural cultural and tourism image creation and dissemination, the development and utilization of rural public cultural spaces face mounting challenges. Novel public cultural spaces emphasize the integration of elements such as aesthetic taste, humanistic care, regional characteristics, and social influence, forming a composite of both public and cultural spaces.[1] China’s 14th Five-Year Plan for Cultural Development calls for the establishment of such novel public cultural spaces, positioning rural public cultural space construction as a pivotal force driving rural revitalization. This not only aims at enhancing the rural image but also seeks to transmit national cultural confidence and traditional essence through the cultivation of cultural spaces.[2]

The Ministry of Culture and Tourism, the National Development and Reform Commission, and the Ministry of Finance, in their opinions on promoting high-quality development of public cultural services, emphasize the innovative expansion of urban and rural public cultural spaces. This involves adapting measures to local conditions and activating excellent rural traditional culture through the construction of thematic spaces like cultural auditoriums, village opera stages, and intangible cultural heritage learning centers.[3] Rural cultural squares, as agglomerations of such thematic spaces, are a significant component in developing new types of rural public cultural spaces, carrying rich regional cultures and exerting considerable social influence.

Tourism and communication are inherently intertwined. Entering the 21st century, the Internet medium has widely and profoundly participated in cultural and tourism communication, rendering new characteristics in rural cultural and tourism dissemination within the information environment.[4] Both cultural squares and libraries, museums cannot detach themselves from the practices of communication under the Internet context, making cultural and tourism communication a fundamental backdrop for exploring the development of new public spaces.

Rural cultural squares represent the most important venues for villagers’ cultural activities, constituting the most utilized and densely populated public cultural spaces. They connect traditional customs with local memories, vividly embodying the cultural features of the countryside and surrounding landscapes.[5] In the practice of cultural and tourism communication, rural squares act as windows showcasing the unique charm of Chinese villages and transmitting historical culture, thereby infusing new cultural connotations into national image building. Therefore, a deep understanding of the role cultural squares play in cultural and tourism communication can contribute to constructing a socially influential Chinese rural image and boosting the country’s cultural soft power.

Against this backdrop, this paper endeavors to explore how cultural squares, as novel public spaces, can empower rural revitalization through cultural and tourism communication practices. What problems arise during this process? How do the driving and interactive roles in cultural tourism affect the construction and development of cultural squares? Have they given rise to new forms of cultural squares? Addressing these questions constitutes an attempt to elevate the empowering capabilities of cultural squares, innovate public cultural spaces, consolidate rural memories, and facilitate cultural and tourism communication.

Literature Review and Theoretical Framework

Literature Review

Over the past century since the emergence of sociology, scholars have ceaselessly engaged in the imagination of space. The concept of space has evolved towards more abstract notions of “social space” following in-depth discussions by French social thinkers. From Bourdieu’s “fields” to Lefebvre’s “production of space,” there is a shared emphasis on the structural nature of social space, while Simmel and subsequent researchers have approached it from the perspective of social interaction, focusing on the influence of social structures and situational interactions on individual behavior.[6] Research in spatial sociology, whether concerning the production-oriented or situational aspects of space, predominantly engages in “ontological inquiries” about space itself.

Spatial Communication Studies

The relationship between communication studies and spatial sociology was first deliberated within the urban ecology framework of the Chicago School. As Chinese communication theory enters a period of reflection and localization, academic practices concerning the connection between communication and rural development have also experienced shifts in direction and approach.[7] Presently, research in communication studies regarding rural public spaces primarily unfolds across three dimensions:

a. Examination of the Publicness of Rural Cultural Spaces and Their Relationship with Rural Governance. Ge Mingsi [8] advocates for actively promoting rural “new infrastructure,” enhancing rural societal mediatization levels, accelerating the cultural construction of county-level integrated media platforms, and innovating the generation of content. Tian Peng [9] argues that the construction of residential areas for rural residents requires breaking free from the constraints of institutional-benefit-driven dynamics and administrative-led practices, reconstructing the integration mechanisms of native societies, allowing farmers to rebuild regional publicness and transition smoothly into social communities. Song Chao et al. [10] address the publicness of rural spaces, proposing an “empowerment through media—reconstruction of publicness—restructuring governance” analytical framework, viewing county-level integrated media platforms as significant carriers of publicness.

b. Exploration of the Image Construction of Rural Public Spaces. Wu Xinyan [11] proposes that the overall image construction of rural public cultural spaces should be guided by the value of cultural confidence, considering spiritual, place-based, and folk craftsmanship cultural spaces. Fu Ting et al. [12], through the study of public reading spaces, suggest that the brand development strategy for rural public spaces requires equal consideration of service demands from various audience groups and the strategic use of multiple channels to enhance brand influence.

c. Focus on the Impact of Online Communication and New Media Development on Rural Public Cultural Spaces. Gu Dazhi et al. [13] argue that the impact of new media erodes fragile rural cultures, necessitating the use of rural cultural spaces for the reconstruction and revival of rural public cultural spaces. Ji Guangxu [14], analyzing青少年 offline social disembedding phenomena from the perspective of social affordances, finds that in the production process of rural public spaces, differences exist in the perceptions and practices among activity subjects, highlighting the need to address both the challenges and opportunities brought by the internet to rural public space formation.

Moreover, spatial communication studies extend to some unique perspectives. Xie Taiping [15], comparing the innovation and diffusion of “rural book houses” and “village square dance,” discovers different communication effects due to varying spatial characteristics in rural public space cultural construction. Li Hongyan et al. [16], through research on participants in rural markets, find that embodied communication imbues tension between village community information and market community information. Despite extensive research, current studies on public space communication largely focus on macroscopic examinations, the roles of space in rural governance, and its function as a medium, with less attention devoted to studying the audiences within these spaces.

Studies on Rural Cultural Squares

Research on typical rural public spaces like cultural auditoriums, village opera stages, and museums is relatively mature. Rural cultural squares, as aggregators of various themes and vital components of new rural public cultural spaces, have increasingly become significant research subjects. Liu Mingtao [17] analyzes rural cultural squares using spatial sociology, suggesting that residents’ voices and their participatory visions for space are being squeezed and require greater inclusion of resident users. Tian Jingli et al. [18] contend that rural squares should increase rural flavor and uniqueness, respecting the strong desires of villagers and reflecting their aspirations for a better life. Ren Hanchen [19], through the study of Huagu Deng culture in Fengtai County, Anhui Province, finds that rural cultural squares serve as an ideal vessel for continuing regional cultural spirit. Overall, research on rural cultural squares under the background of cultural tourism communication is still in its early stages, though planning practices abound, with a primary focus on landscape design and governance issues. Studies within the context of cultural tourism communication pay more attention to rural cultural identity and its transmission.

Through the review of existing literature, it has been observed that research in the fields of communication studies, spatial sociology, and rural public spaces such as cultural squares generally tends to center on “ontological explorations” of space, focusing primarily on the inherent properties and characteristics of these spaces themselves. However, there is a relative dearth of investigation into the intricate interplay between individuals, space, and communication processes. Consequently, this paper devotes particular attention to the driving forces and interactive logics behind the development and construction of cultural squares in the context of cultural tourism communication. It aims to delve into the empowering practice pathways that take into account feedback from the audience, thereby exploring the dynamic relationships between individuals, the spaces they inhabit, and the act of communication within these environments.

Theoretical Framework

The theoretical framework of this paper draws upon two main sources: (1) the “use-experience” perspective in spatial sociology, and (2) the Innovation Diffusion Model.

The “use-experience” perspective in spatial sociology is a conceptual framework developed by Chinese scholars, influenced by Lefebvre’s theory of spatial production and Giddens’ structuration theory in their exploration of the “action-structure” path in spatial research. At its core, this perspective emphasizes people’s practical behaviors and perceptual experiences within space. This viewpoint shifts the focus from conventional spatial structures and forms to actual usage and perception of space, emphasizing its practicality. While Lefebvre asserts that space is a product of social practice, the “use-experience” approach highlights individuals’ practical engagement and sensory experiences within space. Building on Giddens’ structuration theory, which attends to the influence of social structures on individual actions, the “use-experience” theory underscores subjective practices and experiences in space, introducing a stronger subjective dimension. It encompasses three aspects of practical analysis: 1) individuals’ perception, utilization, and interaction with space; 2) interest groups participating in space governance based on their feelings and emotional investment in space; and 3) individuals’ communication activities in networked spaces and related usage behaviors.[20]

This paper does not neglect the “action-structure” theoretical pathways formed by spatial practice and structuration theories, but rather employs them to analyze the status quo of rural cultural squares while using the “use-experience” theory to examine the practices and experiences within these squares, providing a multifaceted consideration of the roles rural cultural squares play in cultural tourism communication.

The Innovation Diffusion Theory emerged in the early 1960s from sociologist Everett Rogers, focusing on explaining how new ideas, products, or practices spread throughout society and influence it. Its key concepts include innovation, adopters, communication channels, social systems, and temporal factors. The theory underscores the social nature of information diffusion and focuses on the adoption process of new practices.

In the broader context of rural revitalization and cultural tourism communication, the Innovation Diffusion Theory provides a profound and comprehensive perspective for analyzing rural cultural squares. This paper will employ the Innovation Diffusion Theory to analyze the functions and cultural connotations of cultural squares, identify the individuals or organizations adopting and embracing their innovations, investigate the relationship between cultural square’s cultural tourism communication, social networks, and cultural inheritance, and explore the most effective pathways for cultural squares in cultural tourism communication.

Spatial sociology emphasizes the practicality and subjective experience of social space, whereas communication studies focus on information transmission and social interaction. Within rural squares, these two disciplines intertwine. Through the “use-experience” theory in spatial sociology, we can deeply analyze individual behavior, perception, and interaction within the square, revealing the mutual shaping of space and social interaction. Under a communication studies lens, we can understand how squares become sites for information transmission, guiding the diffusion of cultural elements. The concepts from spatial sociology offer fresh insights for interpreting the social nature of squares, enabling us to comprehend more fully the multidimensional impacts of rural squares in cultural tourism communication, including social interaction, cultural experiences, and information dissemination.
